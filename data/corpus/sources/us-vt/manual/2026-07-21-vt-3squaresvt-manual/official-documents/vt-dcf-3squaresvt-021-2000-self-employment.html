2000.1 Overview

  1. Self-employment income is earned directly from a business or profession, rather than a specified salary or wages from an employer.
    1. A self-employment business exists when there is no employer/employee relationship. 
    2. Tax and Social Security payments are the business owner’s responsibility. 
    3. Examples include, but are not limited to:
      1.  independent contractors,
      2. landscapers,
      3. dependent care providers,
      4. newspaper carriers,
      5. farmers,
      6. landlords, etc.
  2. Income received from a  Partnership is self-employment income. 
  3. Income received from a  Corporation is self-employment income. Wages and profits paid from a corporation follow normal wage and dividend income calculations. 
  4. Self-employment income is generally reported on income tax forms. See income tax forms. Follow the Procedural Handbook for Determining Self-Employment Income Using Tax Forms. If tax forms are not filed, determine the income from the:
    1. business accounts, 
    2. bookkeeping records, 
    3. sales and expenditures records, or 
    4. other records maintained by the business owner that allows a calculation of the income and expenses. 

2000.2 Resources

  1. Exclude self-employment income from resource calculation during the period for which the income is averaged/annualized.  
  2. Exclude property needed for Income Producing Property and Farm Property self-employment.

2000.3 Income

2000.3.1 Annualizing Income

  1. Budget self-employment income over a 12-month period if the income represents a household’s annual support, even if the income is received in a shorter period of time. If the income is not intended for the household’s annual support, average over the number of months the income is intended to cover. Exception: If a business had a substantial change, budget based on current and Anticipate amounts.  

2000.3.2 Income Calculation

2000.3.2.1 Procedure to Calculate Income

  1. Add all self-employment and  Capital Gain Income, subtract allowable self-employment expenses. This is net self-employment income. Determine the monthly net by dividing by 12 months for annualized income or dividing by the number of months the income is intended to cover. 
  2. Fact sheets, ESD 204 B, Business Income and Expenses;, ESD204F, Farm Income and Expenses, and ESD 204R, Rental Income and Expenses Budgeting, may be used in the income determination. 
  3. Verbal self-declaration may be used in the income determination. CATN the customer's statement. 

2000.3.2.2 New Business

  1. If the business has not yet received income, budget zero. For a new business that has received income, average the income and expenses over the period of time the business has been operating. 

 2000.3.2.3 Substantial Change

  1. If the household reports the averaged or annualized amount does not accurately reflect the household’s actual monthly amounts because of a substantial increase or decrease in the business, budget income based on anticipated amounts. 
  2. Use income and expense amounts for the months after the substantial change and, if needed, add additional amounts the household anticipates, and divide by the number of months used.  

2000.3.2.4 Allowable Self-Employment Expenses

  1. Most costs of producing self-employment income are deducted from the gross business income.  
  2. When the business is located in the home or on the home property, allow only the business portion of the total shelter expense. The self-employment expense is a prorated amount based on the percent of the property used for an office in home or on the home property. 
  3. Exception: Do not allow utilities as a self-employment expense if the business is in the home or on the home property unless the business has a separate meter for the utility.
  4. Note: Allow the household its shelter expenses as a deduction on ACCESS. 

2000.3.2.5 Boarders

  1. Income received for a  Commercial Boarding House is earned income, after allowed self-employment expenses are deducted. 
  2. Income received for non-commercial boarding houses is earned income if the boarder is not included in the 3SVT household. 2000.3.2.6 Dependent Care Providers 

  1. Income received for providing dependent care in the household’s own residence is earned income.

20900.3.2.7 Farm Income

  1. Farm income is derived from cultivating land or crops, or raising animals. Examples include, but are not limited to;
    1. stock,
    2. dairy,
    3. poultry,
    4. fish,
    5. fruit,
    6. ranches,
    7. crops,
    8. nurseries.

2000.3.2.8 Rental Income

  1. Income received from rental property is budgeted as earned income unless a third party is employed to manage the property.  
  2. Income from a  Roomer is rental income. Many separate households live together and  Shared Households expenses. This is not self-employment income.  

2000.3.2.9 Self-Employment Loss

  1. When the expenses are greater than income, the result is a loss.
    1. Do not deduct non-farm losses from any other income.
    2. Deduct farm losses from other net self-employment income.
    3. Deduct any remaining farm loss from the total amount of earned income (minus the earned income deduction) and unearned income.
    4. Exception: Do not budget as a farm loss if the annual gross farm income is less than $1000. 

2000.3.2.10 Income Stops

  1. If the household reports the self-employment has stopped, remove the income. Verbal self-declaration may be used. CATN the household's statement.
